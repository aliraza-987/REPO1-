MHT-CET 2026 PCM — Dual-Session Percentile Analysis

Key Context: For the first time in its recent history, MHT-CET 2026 for the PCM (Physics, Chemistry, Mathematics) category is being conducted in two separate sessions — Session 1 in April and Session 2 in May. This document provides a detailed analysis of expected percentile ranges and how the trends may shift between the two sessions.

2. Impact of the Two-Session Format on Percentile Calculation

In a two-session format, percentiles are typically calculated within each session using an equi-percentile normalisation method. This means:

  • A student's final percentile reflects their relative standing among all test-takers across both sessions after normalisation.
  • If one session has a harder paper, the cut-off raw marks for the same percentile will be lower in that session.
  • If one session attracts stronger students, the competition is more intense, pushing raw cut-offs higher.
Important: Students who appear in both sessions will have their best percentile considered for merit-list calculations.

3. Session 1 — April Attempt Analysis

3.1 Who Tends to Appear in April?

  • Students who are highly prepared and want to secure a good score early.
  • Candidates who have already finished board exam revisions.
  • Students aiming for top engineering colleges who do not want to leave things to a single attempt.

3.2 Paper Difficulty Outlook

The State CET Cell typically keeps the first session slightly more straightforward to set a baseline for normalisation. Historically, early-session papers in other dual-attempt exams (e.g., JEE Main) have been moderate in difficulty.

4. Session 2 — May Attempt Analysis

4.1 Who Tends to Appear in May?

  • Students who are retaking after an unsatisfactory April performance.
  • Students who skipped April and prepared more intensively through April.
  • Board exam candidates who were occupied until late April.

4.2 Paper Difficulty Outlook

The May session paper may be slightly more challenging to compensate for an additional month of preparation time across the candidate pool. This is consistent with patterns observed in JEE Main Session 2 and other dual-attempt national exams.

5. Key Factors That Will Drive the Trend Change

  1. Total Number of Applicants: If 2026 sees a significant increase in PCM applicants (expected due to new engineering colleges and growing awareness), the overall competition will be higher, pushing raw cut-offs up in both sessions.
  2. Board Exam Timing vs. CET Gap: A longer gap between boards and the April CET allows more focused preparation; a shorter gap could slightly suppress scores.
  3. Question Paper Difficulty Calibration: The State CET Cell will try to keep both papers at the same difficulty level. Any deviation gets corrected through normalisation, but raw marks may differ by ±5–8 marks for the same percentile.
  4. Strategic Behaviour — Double Attempt: Top students are likely to appear in both sessions. This will raise the bar in April but may slightly lower the effective competition in May if the same top-scorers have already secured strong scores.
  5. Syllabus & Pattern Stability: Assuming no major syllabus change, score distributions should follow 2025 trends with a marginal upward shift.
